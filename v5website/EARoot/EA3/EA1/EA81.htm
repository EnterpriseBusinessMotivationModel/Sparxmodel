<p>The business model view illustrates the key elements needed to understand the business model.  Note that you do not need to actually have a customer in order to have a business model, but it is often useful, when describing a model, to refer to actual customers to "make it real" to the business stakeholders.  <br/></p><p><br/></p><p>This set of attributes directly relates to the work of Alexander Osterwalder but the relationships are somewhat different in that the EBMM breaks down the attributes based on the needs of an enterprise architecture team to collect the information across an enterprise.  Osterwalder's work is primarily useful for creating business model assessments, so the needs he describes are less rigorous.<br/></p><p><br/></p><p>Note that the Business Model Canvas from Osterwalder's book is a good example of a tool that is useful for discussing (and innovating) on the idea of a business model.  The data collected through that tool could be captured in a data structure built on this metamodel without modification.  In that way, we can say that the Business Model Canvas is a VIEW on this metamodel.<br/></p><p><br/></p><p>The key to understanding the business model viewpoint is to recognize that most of these elements are part of the business model itself. The business model is a unique composition of these elements designed to produce a unique value proposition.  The stakeholders of a business model view would be strategic advisors and company executives seeking to generate new business strategies.<br/></p><p><br/></p><p>For commercial organizations, fully realized business model views are not normally exposed in public except in filings with regulatory bodies (like the Securities and Exchange Commission in the US).  For governmental organizations, these elements are usually described in the legislation and regulations that describe the responsibilities of the organization.<br/></p>
