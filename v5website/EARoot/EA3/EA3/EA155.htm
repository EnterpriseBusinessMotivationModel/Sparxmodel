<p>A business capability taxonomy is an ordered hierarchy of business capabilities, structured in a manner that makes sense to the stakeholders, and used to create associations between capabilities and business units.<br/></p><p><br/></p><p>Note that a relationship to a capability is seen as a relationship to one, many, or all of the capabilities contained within it in the hierarchy.  This ability to make "loose" associations is useful to reduce the difficulty of mapping activities.<br/></p><p><br/></p><p>A business capability is a conceptual "element" of a business.  In many ways, a capability is the basic building block of a business in the same way that atoms and molecules are the building blocks of matter.<br/></p><p><br/></p><p>A business capability is a stable component of the business architecture describing "what" a company does, whereupon a business process describes "how" it does it, and a business model describes "why" it does it.  It is important, when creating a business architectural model, to keep these three interrogatives independent of one another. <br/></p><p><br/></p><p>A business capability is implemented by process, people, technology and information. Business capabilities provide a stable anchor point as process re-engineering, sourcing and technology optimization occur. Linking business capabilities to strategies drives prioritization and focus enabling efficient and effective execution. <br/></p><p><br/></p><p>Note: when discussing the notion of business capabilities to enterprise architects who are familiar with the Zachman Framework, you will <u>not </u>find consensus on where this conceptual element belongs.  This bothers some folks because the Zachman Framework is often compared to the Periodic Table of Elements in chemistry.  Every element must be found in the Periodic table.  Hence, the argument goes, capabilities do not exist because they are not in the table.<br/></p><p><br/></p><p>The authors of the EBMM believe that this argument is simple to address, using the same metaphor.<br/></p><p><br/></p><p>Where the Zachman framework describes the "atoms" of a business, the capabilities are a type of molecule.  And just as the Periodic table of elements does not illustrate a chart of molecules, the Zachman Framework does not illustrate a chart of capabilities.<br/></p><p><br/></p><p>So the next time someone asks "why aren't capabilities in the Zachman Framework," simply reply with a similar question: "why isn't caffeine in the Periodic Table of Elements?"  <br/></p>
