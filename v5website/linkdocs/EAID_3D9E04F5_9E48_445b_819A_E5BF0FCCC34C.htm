Enterprise Business Motivation Model
Model Documentation


Introduction

This document describes the Enterprise Business Motivation Model (EBMM), a

conceptual model of Enterprise Business Architecture.  The EBMM was

originally developed by Nick Malik, Enterprise Architect from Microsoft, with

inputs and collaboration from dozens of architects, innovators, business

leaders, and technology practitioners across a wide array of industries.  The

EBMM was first published in 2009 in an article in the Architecture Journal and

was subsequently revised in the spring and summer of 2011.  Subsequent

revisions have taken place with other architects, notably including Mike Clark

and Robert Singers.

Purpose of the Enterprise Business Motivation Model

As of the first decade of the millennium, Business Architecture has become a

profession in its own right, emerging from a wide array of business

management practices including leadership, organizational design, and

enterprise architecture.  Early practices emerged out of consulting

organizations, and were created in a proprietary context.  Each of the

practitioners worked for the same company, and shared both the language and

culture of that company.

The people who worked as Business Architecture consultants were fairly fluid,

moving from one company to another and taking their practices and

experiences with them.  As a result, while the practices of Business

Architecture have emerged from many companies, the practices themselves

are quite similar in scope and intended effect.

To the dismay of many practitioners of Business Architecture, each consulting

firm uses a different language set when describing the terminology of business

architecture.  As a result, while the practices are quite similar, the profession

itself has a difficult time maturing, as findings and conclusions developed in

one company have to be painstakingly translated to the terminology of other

organizations to be reused or improved upon.

The goal of the Enterprise Business Motivation Model is nothing less than to

be the Rosetta Stone for Business Architecture.  By describing the common

concerns of Business Architecture, and then illustrating how the terminology

can be used to address those concerns, the EBMM attempts to form the

foundation for shared communication and collaboration across the field. 

There are many potential benefits for creating and supporting a shared

language for Business Architecture.  Training can become consistent and

aligned with other fields, practitioners can consume the artifacts produced by

others more easily, and therefore deliver value more quickly to our

stakeholders, and the credibility of the entire field can be improved as

consistent leaders emerge, able to communicate with stakeholders in many

organizations.

Scope of the Enterprise Business Motivation Model

Creating a project to define the terms and terminology of Business Architecture

is a mammoth undertaking.  The field has emerged organically across a wide

array of practitioners, each who brings their own terms and practices to the

field.  The scope of this effort must be carefully defined in order to prevent the

effort from becoming bogged down in indirect-yet-related concerns.

The scope of this effort will focus on the interrelationship of concepts, and the

information that should be understood when a concept is used.  In effect, the

model helps practitioners to speak consistently about the structure and shape

of an enterprise.

Words by themselves are not interesting, and therefore not memorable.  In

order for the concepts of business architecture to be used consistently, we

must tie those concepts to practices, methods, and artifacts that business

architects follow and produce each and every day.  Concepts cannot live

independently of the processes and methods in which they are used, because

these methods define the community of people who share the concepts. 

Therefore, the following elements are "in-scope" for the EBMM documentation

that follows:

  • A basic understanding of key concerns of business architecture, and a

    simple taxonomy of business roles that may share those concerns.  This

    work helps to clarify the stakeholders for the EBMM and the concerns that

    they would like to see addressed through business architecture.

  • A reasonable set of "viewpoints" to approach the concerns of the

    stakeholders.  A viewpoint, as described in ISO 42010, is a coherent

    intersection of stakeholder concerns.  By understanding the viewpoints we

    seek to address, business architects can focus in on a subset of concepts

    needed to address them.  For each viewpoint, a subset of the entire EBMM

    can be described and used.

  • A rationalized list of terms for the entire Enterprise Business Motivation

    Model. 

The following elements are "out of scope" for the EBMM documentation that

follows:

  • A complete description of the methods, processes, and tasks of business

    architecture.  While this would be useful, and is a necessary part of

    creating a common base of performance for the field, it is simply too

    difficult for the people involved in creating this model to gain sufficient

    consensus to describe these methods in any authoritative manner.

  • A complete rationalization of terms pertaining to every other field that

    business architects may interact with.  The terminology of Business

    Architecture has emerged from individual innovators, leveraging their own

    experience as well as their synthesis of the published works of innovative

    management thinkers, ranging from Peter Drucker to Rummler and Brache.

    These thinkers did not work together to create their world view, and the

    resulting synthesis that emerges in the work of Business Architecture is

    similarly "free flowing" with concepts and concerns that do not directly fall

    within the scope of BA.  We are not going to attempt to get these business

    leaders, or their acolytes, to agree on terms and terminology.  That would

    be unnecessary to meet our goals.

  • A technical data model useful for creating products.  This conceptual model

    is not a technical data model.  Many various data models can be derived

    from the full EBMM or from the viewpoint models.  Those models can be

    used to create software products.  While this is a very valuable endeavor, it

    is outside the scope of this effort.  The license for the EBMM provides

    anyone who would like to view it the full rights to create such software

    components without royalties to any contributors to the model.  This is

    effectively an open source effort.  However, the value of creating a model

    for the sake of communication cannot be reached in a reasonable

    timeframe if we allow ourselves to become consumed with the necessary

    but tedious details of relational data modeling.  We leave that level of

    analysis to others.

Who is involved

The effort of creating the EBMM will not be limited to people directly involved in

business architecture.  However, we will seek out contributions from business

architects and enterprise architects as a first order activity and the

contributions of actual practitioners will be viewed more highly and considered

more readily than the contributions of interested individuals who do not

participate in business architecture activities as part of their vocation.

That said, there is no "governing body" for the EBMM at this time.  There is not

"committee of peers" that is actively formed and regularly meeting to discuss

this effort.  It emerges over time.  As each potential contributor discovers the

EBMM and submits BA-specific ideas for consideration, Nick Malik, the primary

author of the EBMM, will take those considerations into account and either

modify the model or provide a good justification for how the existing model can

meet that expectation.  As a result, there is no end date for "completing" the

model.  We should all expect it to gradually emerge over the span of many

years.

If, over time, a governing body emerges that would like to collaborate to

develop and refine the model, the above will change.
